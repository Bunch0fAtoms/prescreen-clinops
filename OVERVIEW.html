1 The goal

Match more patients to trials, without ever exposing PHI

The goal: get more eligible patients in front of the trials they qualify for, and prove their data stayed protected at every step. One real Fred Hutch problem, sized to build in two days.

The scenario

A coordinator is screening breast-cancer patients for a set of trials. The rules are clear enough: a biomarker status, an age range, a prior therapy. But the evidence is not all in the structured tables. For many patients, HER2 or ER status was only ever typed into a free-text pathology note, so a query over the structured data silently skips them. Each one is a patient who might have qualified, and never got the call.

That gap is what you close. Recover the patients hidden in the notes, so more people reach the trials they qualify for, and because this is real patient data, keep it governed the whole way so a researcher only ever sees what they are entitled to.

What you build on

You start from six synthetic OMOP tables, person, condition_occurrence, measurement, observation, drug_exposure, and note, holding 300 breast-cancer patients. They carry the same column names and types as Fred Hutch's real curated_omop.omop schema, so moving from synthetic to real data is a change in configuration, not a rewrite. Some patients are clearly eligible for a trial, some clearly are not, and some surface only when you read the notes. That is the build scenario, and it is what makes the two days worth it.

4 The governed foundation

Governance is the foundation everyone inherits

It is not a separate track. It is the layer under all four, set once and inherited by everything built on top.

One policy, set high, inherited everywhere

Classified and access-controlled, before anything is built on top

Together we stand up the shared synthetic OMOP data, then the governance team classifies it and sets a tag-based policy at the catalog and schema level. They set it once, high in the hierarchy. Everything the Data Engineering and Applied AI teams compose on top, new tables, a registered model, inherits that governance automatically, and any new column that carries a PHI tag is masked the moment it appears, with nothing to re-apply. The six synthetic OMOP tables carry the same names as your real curated_omop.omop, so the same work repoints at real data with one switch, whenever you are ready and approved.

classify → tag the sensitive columns mask → hide values by group tag-based policy → follows the tag catalog-wide synthetic → real → one switch
